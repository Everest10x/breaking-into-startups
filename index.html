The RoadMap

Artur Meyster on the left and Timur Meyster on the right.

Who we are:

In 2014, my identical twin, Artur Meyster and I, decided to leave our corporate jobs in investment banking and project management in order to become engineers. Less than two years later, we received numerous six figure offers from top Silicon Valley Startups to work as full-time software engineers. Regardless of whether you are trying to become an engineer or build your own start-up, the resources below describe the path we took to learn how to program - the tutorials, the blogs, and the books that helped us acquire this invaluable skill set. In case you have doubts that you too can make this jump, we both were finance majors in college and we began this journey from scratch.

How to get started:

Knowing what we know today, this is the advice we would of given ourselves in 2014. There are a lot of tutorials for beginners that attempt to teach you the fundemantals, however, a lot of the time you walk away learning a subset of the language or technology, without grasping the full picture of the skillset required to build a functioning website. Whether you want to start your own company or work as an engineer, you need to understand how all the different pieces fit together. The one resource that put all together for us was the One Month Rails course. You can get a free trial for free at OneMonthRails.

After about five months of completing this course and the tutorials listed below, I applied to App Academy and my brother, Artur, applied to Hack Reactor. Even though we were spending weeknights and weekends learning how to code, from our research we concluded that going through a coding bootcamp with accelerate our learning and teach the technologies necessary to enter the work field. From speaking with several engineers, we learned that there is a huge difference between building a person project and working on a team of developers who are deploying production grade code on daily basis. The bootcamps would expose us to the industry accepted tools, frameworks and testing methodologies that are used by engineers at the best startups.

Common Pitfalls:

  • Not finishing a tutorial because you feel like you are not grasping the concepts fast enough. Most people don't realize that a lot of beginners have to take the same tutorial 2-3 times before these concepts start to sink in. The first time you are just following the steps. The second time you are able to recognize patterns. The third time is when you actually understand how you would apply it.
  • Running into bugs and errors is frustrating, especially if you are just starting out. Most people don’t realize that 75% of the engineering time is spent debugging the code by googling errors or asking for help on Stack Overflow. To succeed you have to embrace the struggle and count that time spent as your learning experience.
  • Don’t be afraid of the unknown. No one start out knowing how to program, its a process that takes years to perfect. Great engineers look forward to solving challenging problems they haven’t encountered before. If nothing else, this mindset will keep you moving forward until one day you will look back at those problems and laugh how easy they were.
